The Convenience of Illusion

Nicole S Kadi

Imagine you could live your life experiencing the greatest pleasures of the world. All your dreams and greatest desires would come true, whether it's making groundbreaking scientific discoveries or becoming a world-renowned politician. The imaginations are endless. This is what Robert Nozick's experience machine offers: a chance to live your greatest, most pleasurable life. But what of reality? But would you choose this reality? More importantly, what does your answer reveal about how committed you truly are to reality?

In Anarchy, State, and Utopia (1974), Nozick introduces this thought experiment to counter hedonism. Hedonism is the philosophical view that prioritizes pleasure and avoids pain, claiming that humans are motivated to seek pleasure over pain. In his thought experiment, he poses the question: would you plug into this machine for the rest of your life, knowing you would experience the maximum amount of pleasure your life could bring you? ... Gossiping Tweens and Ending Regimes: The Promises and Pitfalls of the Doctrine of Double Effect

Karis E Morasch

Each day of our lives, we are required to weigh the good and bad of our choices and make the decision we choose as best. Oftentimes, we don't judge the morality of our actions based on any particular framework, but rather use our intuitive judgements to the best of our ability. The task of moral philosophers is to create a framework that aligns with our intuitive judgements that will allow us to retrospectively or proactively gauge the morality of a particular action. Several of these moral frameworks focus on the balance of good and bad in the result of a particular action to determine moral praiseworthy or blameworthiness of the action, a view known as consequentialism. While this may seem like a comprehensive system at first glance, it fails to account for another facet of actions that we intuitively know holds moral weight: intentions. We would not hold the person who engages in a morally blameworthy action while under threat of physical harm to the same standard as someone who committed an identical action completely of their own volition, even though the outcomes are the same. ... Life Simulators

Vienna Gaspar

In the 1981 post-structuralist book Simulacra and Simulation, Jean Baudrillard puts forward his theory that signs have come to constitute and replace reality. The term ‘simulacra’ (plural: simulacrum) refers to representations and copies that have either come to replace reality itself, or never had originals. ‘Simulation’ simply refers to something which emulates real life. Baudrillard proposes his theory of the four stages of simulacra which mark a degradation of reality:

1 It is the reflection of a basic reality.
2 It masks and perverts a basic reality.
3 It masks the absence of a basic reality.
4 It bears no relation to any reality whatever: it is its own pure simulacrum.

Does Liberalism Understand People?

Max Abubucker

The relationship between citizen and State is not one-sided. Individuals are shaped by the social and political systems that they live in. Our society plays a large part in our desires, fears, and conception of ourselves. Analogously, political systems seek to govern individuals. Therefore, in seeking to justify a political system, a political philosophy must rely on a conception of human nature.

The political system of the United States is liberal democracy. In fact, the United States was the first nation founded with an explicitly liberal government. Liberalism is a political philosophy that places individual rights and freedom above all else. Since the end of World War II, liberalism has been the dominant political philosophy of Western nations. Recently, however, liberalism in the United States and across the globe has been failing. We are experiencing widespread discontent with public institutions and the rise of populism and authoritarianism. Our Obsession with the Internal

Deniz Durusoy

No one familiar with the history of "Weird Fiction" can say that the books of the genre have as important of a role in our lives as they did at the end of the 19th century. Back in the good old days when death by Polio was a common occurrence and the world was getting ready for the World War(s), the Gothic tradition matured into what we call today the genre of horror. It was writers such as Edgar Allan Poe, E.T.A Hoffman and their sometimes gruesome or dreadful other times thought provoking stories which shaped what we call today as fiction in general. Nevertheless, those days are long past. Apart from the rare sighting of a horror geek (such as myself) one would rarely see anyone interested in "Weird fiction" or be aware of the writings of the controversial Lovecraft or the underappreciated Ramsey Campbell. The inability of this once significant genre to find a place as it once did in our media intake signifies a great change in what we want to consume in general.
